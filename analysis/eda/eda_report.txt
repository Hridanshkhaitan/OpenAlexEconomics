EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS REPORT
OpenAlex Economics Corpus (field 20: Economics, Econometrics and Finance)
8,646,207 works, publication years 1647-2025
================================================================================
Audience: economics research team (professors + RA) deciding how to use this corpus.
Tone: numbers-first, neutral. Every figure traces to a verified analysis dimension.
Where adversarial verification corrected a number, the corrected value is used and flagged.


1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The fifteen most decision-relevant facts:

 1. SIZE AND SHAPE. The corpus is 8,646,207 works across 376 parquet files, one file per
    year (1647-2025), 17 columns each. It is heavily recent: 85.00% of works are dated
    1990 or later, 76.70% are 2000+, 58.05% are 2010+; pre-1900 is just 86,705 works (1.0%).

 2. PEAK AND PLATEAU. The peak output year is 2016 (358,265 works), just ahead of 2023
    (356,523) and 2020 (353,057). Output has plateaued near 350k/yr since 2016, not saturated:
    long-run growth was 5.46%/yr (1950-2019) and accelerated to 5.96%/yr (2000-2019).

 3. RECENT COUNTS ARE INGESTION ARTIFACTS. The 2020-2022 dip (353,057 -> 279,702, -20.8%,
    2022 the lowest year since 2012) and the +27.5% 2023 rebound (356,523) are driven by
    type-specific ingestion, not real output change: 2023 rode an all-time-high 90,725 book
    chapters, and dissertation indexing collapsed (26,428 in 2019 -> 6,406 in 2022).
    2024-2025 declines are indexing lag. Do not read recent-year counts as scholarly trends.

 4. ABSTRACTS ARE THE BINDING NLP CONSTRAINT. abstract is NULL for 51.88% of rows
    (4,485,184) - the single largest gap. Coverage never exceeds ~59% in any year and is
    only 54.6% even in the 2020s. Critically, availability is PUBLISHER-DETERMINED, not
    random: Elsevier econ/finance journals sit near 5-10% abstract coverage (Journal of
    Banking & Finance 4.6%, Journal of Econometrics 5.8%) versus 96%+ for OUP/T&F titles.

 5. CITATIONS ARE EXTREME. Median cited_by_count is 0 for EVERY publication year 1980-2025;
    70.81% of all works (6,122,578) are uncited, still 69.44% among mature (<=2015) works.
    The top 1% of works hold 53.39% of all 55.9M citations; only 95 works exceed 10,000 cites.

 6. CITATION EDGES ARE MOSTLY ABSENT. referenced_works_count = 0 for 75.94% of rows
    (6,566,158) - even 70.9% of journal articles - overwhelmingly a metadata-coverage gap,
    not reference-free scholarship. The parquet keeps only the COUNT, not the edges, so
    citation-network work requires re-extracting referenced_works from the raw archive.

 7. VENUES ARE NOT JOURNALS. The five largest "journals" are SSRN (274,089), RePEc
    (172,246), PubMed (127,958), OUP eBooks (75,345) and Columbia Academic Commons (63,085) -
    all repositories/aggregators/databases. Publisher eBook series alone are 7.58% of the
    corpus. journal strings are unusable as-is for venue-level analysis.

 8. TOPIC CATCH-ALL. Of 107 primary_topics, one bucket - "Diverse Scientific and Economic
    Studies" - holds 25.24% of the whole corpus (2,182,294 works). It is simultaneously a
    garbage magnet AND home to top-tier AER papers, so it cannot simply be dropped.

 9. NON-ECONOMICS CONTAMINATION. Hard, unambiguous non-economics leakage (clinical medicine,
    pure media/humanities, one metadata glitch) is ~5-8% of the corpus; a broader definition
    that also removes health-services/health-economics work pushes plausible non-core content
    to ~13.33% (1,152,831 works: suspect venue OR clearly-clinical topic).

10. TEAM SIZE IS RISING BUT ECON STAYS SMALL. Mean author_count rose from ~1.30 (1900s) to
    2.33 (2020s); the median moved from 1 to 2 only in the 2020s. Small teams (2-3 authors)
    out-cite solo authors 3.2x (mean 15.49 vs 4.91 cites, 2000-2015), with an inverted-U:
    4+ author papers do slightly worse (14.56), unlike lab sciences.

11. MISSING AUTHORSHIP IS MODERN, NOT ANCIENT. 22.93% of works (1,982,458) have
    author_count = 0; their median year is 2011 (1.40M post-2000, only 137k pre-1950).
    first_author IS NULL identifies EXACTLY these same rows (0 divergence).

12. JOURNAL FURNITURE. ~5.48% of works (473,537) have furniture titles ("Editorial Board"
    x330,437, "Table of Contents" x167,705, etc.); a fuller cleaning pattern plus type =
    paratext flags 986,638 works (11.41%) as removable non-research material. Type filtering
    alone is insufficient - 12,923 "Editorial Board" items are typed article, not paratext.

13. PROVENANCE INTEGRITY IS EXCELLENT. Identity/classification fields have zero NULLs
    (id, type, publication_date, publication_year, field, subfield, primary_topic, is_oa,
    cited_by_count, referenced_works_count, author_count). Dates are perfectly consistent:
    0 NULL, 0 unparseable, 0 rows where year(publication_date) != publication_year.

14. PRE-1900 IS DIGITIZATION NOISE. The pre-1800 slice (3,899 works) is 50.2% OUP eBooks
    (Herrick's 1647 Hesperides poems, Tatler/Spectator issues); the 19th century (82,806
    works) is 26.9% Notes and Queries plus Lancet/BMJ. Treat pre-1900 (arguably pre-1940)
    as topic-classifier noise for substantive analyses.

15. RICH UNEXTRACTED DATA EXISTS. The raw 49-field JSON archive holds ~32 fields the parquet
    dropped - referenced_works (citation edges), affiliations/countries, disambiguated author
    IDs, full topic lists, FWCI and citation percentiles, counts_by_year, funders, open-access
    detail. Any citation-network, collaboration, or normalized-impact study should re-extract
    from data/archive/ rather than work from the 17-column parquet alone.

Open-access footnote (from is_oa, 0 NULLs): OA share climbed 15.6% (1990) -> 20.0% (2000) ->
26.0% (2010) -> 43.4% (2020) -> 60.4% (2025), steepest after 2015.


2. DATASET PROVENANCE AND STRUCTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Source and scope
- OpenAlex "works" filtered to primary_topic.field = "Economics, Econometrics and Finance"
  (OpenAlex field 20).
- 8,646,207 works spanning publication years 1647-2025.
- Downloaded via the OpenAlex API over 2026-07-10 to 2026-07-13.

On-disk layout
- Stored as data/parquet/<century>/<year>.parquet: 376 files total, one per year, 1647-2025
  ascending. Each file has 16 base columns plus publication_year (17 columns).
- The 17 columns are: id, type, publication_date, publication_year, field, subfield,
  primary_topic, is_oa, cited_by_count, referenced_works_count, author_count, abstract, doi,
  first_author, journal, language, title.
- Raw 49-field JSON archives are retained separately in data/archive/ (e.g. econ_2020.jsonl.gz).
  The parquet is therefore a 17-of-49-field projection; ~32 top-level raw fields were not
  extracted (inventory in Section 4).

Structural integrity (full-corpus, all 8,646,207 rows)
- ZERO NULLs in: id, type, publication_date, publication_year, field, subfield,
  primary_topic, is_oa, cited_by_count, referenced_works_count, author_count.
- Date consistency is perfect: 0 NULL publication_date, 0 unparseable dates (TRY_CAST), and
  0 rows where year(publication_date) differs from publication_year.
- Missingness in text fields is encoded purely as NULL (no stray empty strings) EXCEPT title,
  which has both NULL (2,423) and whitespace-empty (4,798) values.

What the row grain is
- One row per OpenAlex work id. Known duplication exists at the WORK level for the same
  intellectual object (e.g. North's 1990 book appears as two work ids; JAMA appears under two
  journal-name strings) - see Sections 5-6. The parquet is not de-duplicated to the
  publication level.

Growth
- The 1940s is the only decade since 1800 smaller than its predecessor (-2.7% vs 1930s).
- CAGR of annual output: 5.46%/yr for 1950 (8,364 works) -> 2019 (326,523), and 5.96%/yr for
  2000 (108,680) -> 2019. Growth accelerated in the 2000s; the corpus is not saturating.
- Recency concentration: 85.00% of the corpus is 1990+, 76.70% is 2000+, 58.05% is 2010+.

Peak, plateau, and the recent non-monotonicity
- All-time peak year: 2016 (358,265), then 2023 (356,523), then 2020 (353,057).
- The 2016 spike is a book-chapter ingestion event: chapters doubled 35,412 (2015) -> 70,805
  (2016); output then slid -8.9% through 2019 (326,523).
- 2020 rebounded to 353,057 (its 206,573 articles are the highest article count of any year),
  then fell -11.1% (2021: 314,006) and -10.9% (2022: 279,702, lowest since 2012), before
  2023 jumped +27.5% to 356,523 on an all-time-high 90,725 book chapters (vs 46,415 in 2022).
- A dissertation-indexing collapse amplifies the 2021-22 dip: 26,428 dissertations in 2019
  vs 6,406 in 2022.
- CONCLUSION: recent non-monotonicity is a type-specific ingestion/indexing-lag artifact,
  not real output decline. 2024-2025 declines (figures show 319,078 / 312,644) are lag.

Historical dips (these are real)
- WWI cut annual output -22.7% (1913: 3,999 -> 1918: 3,091; recovered by 1921).
- WWII cut it -21.5% (1938: 6,725 -> 1944: 5,279).
- The 1970-1971 drop (-12.3%, 25,096 -> 22,015) is the largest single-year drop between 1952
  and 2016 - but 1970 is INFLATED by repository bulk-dating (e.g. University of Patras
  repository has 671 works dated 1970, Morehead State 590, vs 171 in 1971): a "default year"
  dump, not a real 1971 collapse.

Type mix since 1900 (top 6)
- Article share fell 83.7% (1900s) -> 71.0% (1990s) -> 60.3% (2010s) -> 59.2% (2020s).
- Book-chapters rose 6.3% -> 16.8% (2020s); dissertations 0.3% -> 6.3% (2010s); preprints
  ~0.1-0.3% (pre-1990) -> 5.3% (2020s); paratext holds 3-7% throughout; books declined
  5.3% -> 1.6%.

Team size over time
- Average author_count was flat near 1.30 from the 1900s through the 1950s (1.295 -> 1.297),
  then climbed every decade: 1.35 (1960s), 1.42 (1970s), 1.49 (1980s), 1.63 (1990s),
  1.92 (2000s), 1.95 (2010s), 2.33 (2020s).
- The median was 1 in every decade until the 2020s, when it became 2. Solo authorship lost
  its majority only in this decade. (NOTE: these averages restrict to author_count > 0;
  raw all-work means look lower - e.g. 0.80 in 1950 - because author_count=0 records exist.)

Citation aging curve (avg cited_by_count by publication year)
- Rises with age, peaking for works published 1990 (15.18) and 1999 (15.12), then declines:
  4.01 (2016), ~3.9-4.7 (2017-2021), 2.25 (2023), 1.45 (2024), 0.49 (2025).
- Median cited_by_count is 0 for EVERY year 1980-2025 - most economics-field works are never
  cited regardless of vintage.
- Zero-cited share by 5-year bucket is U-shaped: 68.6% (1980-84), 65.8% (1985-89),
  63.9% (1990-94), 61.8% (1995-99, the minimum), 63.7% (2000-04), 67.5% (2005-09),
  70.0% (2010-14), 72.8% (2015-19), 70.7% (2020-24), 85.7% (2025). Rising uncitedness after
  2000, despite less-aged competition, reflects corpus dilution by low-citation types
  (dissertations, paratext, datasets, libguides).

Pre-1900 = digitization artifact, not economics
- The 222 "1647" works are Robert Herrick's Hesperides poems digitized as OUP eBook chapters.
- The 1709-1712 spike is Tatler/Spectator issues; the 1727 spike (606 works) is a numbered
  Latin correspondence collection (592 with null journal).
- Of 3,899 pre-1800 works, 83.1% are typed book-chapter; OUP eBooks alone = 1,958 (50.2%),
  then null journal (1,359) and Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society (450).
- The 19th century (82,806 works) is dominated by Notes and Queries (22,251 = 26.9%),
  The Lancet (6,140) and BMJ (4,981) - antiquarian and medical journals misclassified into
  field 20.
- GUIDANCE: treat anything pre-1900, and arguably pre-1940, as topic-classifier noise for
  substantive analyses.

Coverage takeaways
- DOI coverage is U-shaped: WORST in the 2000s (55.2%), best in the 2020s (89.1%, reaching
  95.9% in 2023). Pre-1900 works are surprisingly well-DOI'd (85.3% of 86,705 rows) from
  retro-digitized journals.
- Abstract coverage never exceeds ~59% in any year and bottoms at 29.6% in the 1960s.
- Journal coverage dips to 77.1% in the 2010s, driven by repository/preprint growth - the
  missing-venue problem is worst exactly in the modern period most analyses will target.
- LANGUAGE COVERAGE COLLAPSES AT THE RECENT EDGE. NULL share: 1.17% (2019), 1.13% (2020),
  1.35% (2021), 3.43% (2022), 9.47% (2023), 10.72% (2024), 20.31% (2025). Within 2023-25 it
  hits articles (18.4% null) and conference papers (27.2%) hardest - consistent with
  OpenAlex language detection lagging newly ingested records. Any language-filtered analysis
  will SILENTLY DROP about a fifth of 2025.

The ten most research-valuable unextracted fields, and what each would enable:
 (1) referenced_works - the actual W-id citation edges; enables full citation networks,
     knowledge-flow and intellectual-lineage analysis (parquet keeps only the count).
 (2) authorships[].institutions + countries - affiliation, country, and international-

     single is_oa boolean.
 (8) funders/grants + awards - funding-acknowledgment analysis of economics research.
 (9) biblio (volume/issue/pages) - page-count proxies for article length; precise citation.
(10) primary_location.source issn/host_organization/is_in_doaj - stable journal identifiers
     (the journal string alone is ambiguous - see Section 6) and publisher-level rollups.
Honorable mentions: is_retracted (integrity filtering), indexed_in, corresponding_author_ids,

5. CITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Distribution (extraordinarily skewed)
- Overall: mean 6.47, median 0, p75 = 1, p90 = 8, p99 = 108, max = 70,886.
- Works published 2000+ (n = 6,631,707): mean 5.36, median 0, p75 = 1, p90 = 7, p99 = 93,
  max = 37,204.
- The median economics work has never been cited; even the 75th percentile has exactly 1 cite.

Zero-citation share (not just recency)
- 70.81% of all works (6,122,578) have 0 citations.
- Still 69.44% among mature works (publication_year <= 2015, n = 5,350,246), and 67.21%
  among mature journal articles only - so two-thirds of even mature articles are uncited.
- Zero-cite share by type (n >= 1000): minimum is review at 36.05% (report 44.64%,
  book 46.52%), rising through book-chapter ~84%, dissertation ~91%, dataset ~94%, to
  paratext 96.90% and a maximum of libguides 99.95% (peer-review 98.41%, conference-paper
  98.02%). Part of the corpus-wide 71% is genuinely non-citable material, but the uncited
  long tail of articles is a real feature (low-visibility venues).
  [Correction: an earlier draft framed the type range as "46.5% (books) to 96.9% (paratext)";
   verification shows the true extremes are review 36.05% (low) and libguides 99.95% (high).]

Concentration
- Total citations across the corpus = 55,930,164.
- Top 1% of works (86,462) hold 29,860,371 citations = 53.39% of all citations.
- Top 0.1% (8,646) hold 13,231,724 = 23.66%.
- Only 95 works have >= 10,000 citations; only 4,022 have >= 1,000 (0.047% of the corpus).

References (referenced_works_count)
- Mean 6.51, median 0, 75.94% zero overall.
- Zero-ref share by decade: 1950s 96.1%, 1960s 94.5%, 1970s 89.8%, 1980s 83.3%, 1990s 79.5%,
  2000s 76.1%, 2010s 74.3%, 2020s 71.9%.
- This is overwhelmingly a COVERAGE artifact, not reference-free scholarship. Even restricting
  to journal articles, the zero-ref share for the 2020s is about 56.5% (type='article',
  year>=2020) - implausibly high for genuinely reference-free work, so OpenAlex simply lacks
  parsed reference lists for most non-mainstream venues, with coverage decaying going back.
  [Correction: an earlier "61.48% of 2020s journal articles" figure did not reproduce on
   verification; the true value under every reasonable definition is ~54.6-57.4%, ~56.5%.]
- Genuinely reference-free types behave as expected: paratext 97.5% zero, datasets 98.6%,
  libguides 100%.
- Among works WITH references the numbers look like real bibliographies: mean 27.1, median 18,
  and articles' mean nonzero references grew 5.5 (1950s) -> 29.2 (2020s).

Team size vs citations (2000-2015 cohort)
- A naive "solo" bucket is contaminated by 651,949 works with author_count = 0 (missing
  authorship) averaging just 1.03 cites (93.8% uncited). Excluding those:
    solo (n = 1,641,616):     mean 4.91 cites, 71.1% uncited
    2-3 authors (n = 847,100): mean 15.49, median 1, 46.9% uncited
    4+ authors (n = 195,081):  mean 14.56, 51.8% uncited
- Small teams out-cite solo authors 3.2x, with an INVERTED-U: 4+ author works do slightly
  worse than 2-3 - unlike lab sciences, big-team economics papers gain no further citation
  premium. Part of the solo/team gap is compositional (solo includes more notes, comments,
  theses, and local-language work).


6. VENUES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Coverage
- 1,527,829 works (17.67%) have a NULL journal. The NULL rate is strongly time-varying:
  1900s 13.0%, 1910s 14.3%, 1920s 17.4%, 1930s 11.8%, 1940s 8.0% (best), 1950s 11.0%,
  1960s 13.1%, 1970s 14.1%, 1980s 10.8%, 1990s 12.5%, 2000s 17.9%, 2010s 22.9% (worst;
  per-year peak 25.3% in 2013), 2020-2025 14.8% (falling to 12.5-13.5% by 2022-2025).
- So missing venue is worst exactly in the modern period.

Diversity and concentration
- 135,738 distinct journal-name strings.
- Top 100 names hold 2,000,294 works = 23.13% of the corpus (28.10% of the 7,118,378 works
  with a non-null journal).
- Long tail: 34,057 names (25.1% of all names) have exactly one work.

Top venues are mostly NOT journals
- By work count: SSRN Electronic Journal 274,089 (3.17%), RePEc 172,246 (1.99%),
  PubMed 127,958 (1.48%), Oxford University Press eBooks 75,345 (0.87%), Columbia Academic
  Commons 63,085 (0.73%). The first genuinely traditional periodical in the top-25 is
  Notes and Queries (#12, 37,276) - itself an off-topic literary-history journal.
- By SUM of citations: SSRN 2,115,547; The Journal of Finance 1,222,756 (8,706 works);
  American Economic Review 1,021,821 (5,420); NBER 879,454; RePEc 871,323; Econometrica
  810,908 (3,552); JPE 716,893; Journal of Financial Economics 700,105 (2,621 works -
  highest cites/work at ~267); QJE 686,293; The Economic Journal 637,992.
- SSRN is #1 on BOTH rankings (largest by works and by citations).

Quality-heuristic patterns (ILIKE on journal)

Name-variant duplicates
- GOOD: for 11 classic econ journals tested, OpenAlex has already normalized "The X" vs "X" to

Non-economics venue leakage in the top 100
- 13 clearly non-econ medical/science venues (PubMed, BMJ, JAMA x2, Lancet, Science, Nature,

(a) exclude NULL + repository/eBook/database sources for journal analyses; (b) exact-match the
canonical "The X" forms for flagship journals; (c) treat corrupt sources (Section 10) as junk.

- 4,161,023 works (48.13%) have an abstract; when present, mean length 136.4 words, median 116.
- Coverage rises monotonically by decade: 1950s 31.7%, 1960s 29.6% (trough), 1970s 33.3%,
  1980s 35.4%, 1990s 41.7%, 2000s 47.2%, 2010s 52.0%, 2020s 54.6%.
- Among the top-5 "journals" by size: SSRN 15.7% abstract coverage, RePEc 92.3%, PubMed 24.0%,
  OUP eBooks 19.6%, Columbia Academic Commons 1.4%.
- The PUBLISHER EFFECT among named journals is dramatic: every Elsevier title sampled has
  near-zero coverage - Journal of Banking & Finance 4.6%, Journal of Econometrics 5.8%,
  Economics Letters 6.2%, World Development 6.5%, Energy Economics 9.7% - versus Applied
  Economics (T&F) 96.6%, QJE (OUP) 96.3%, The Economic Journal 79.5%, AER 70.0%, Econometrica
  50.6%, Journal of Finance 39.3%, JPE 24.7%. Elsevier withholds abstracts from OpenAlex's
  inverted index, so any abstract-based text analysis will SYSTEMATICALLY EXCLUDE core
  Elsevier econ/finance journals - a first-order selection bias to document.

Cleaning bottom line: drop paratext + furniture-title works (~11.4% of corpus); treat
post-2022 language NULLs as metadata lag not trend; never treat abstract presence as random -
it is publisher-determined.


8. TOPIC CLASSIFICATION AND NON-ECON LEAKAGE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Subfield structure (only 3 values)
    Economics and Econometrics                       6,688,369  (77.36%)
    Finance                                          1,084,315  (12.54%)
    General Economics, Econometrics and Finance        873,523  (10.10%)
- Subfield is too coarse (3 values) to be a useful filter.

Topic structure (107 primary_topics, extremely top-heavy)
- "Diverse Scientific and Economic Studies" alone = 2,182,294 works (25.24% of the corpus) -
  a catch-all magnet.
- Next largest: Healthcare Policy and Management (259,869; 3.01%), Global trade and economics
  (210,768; 2.44%), Economic Theory and Policy (181,999; 2.10%), Historical Economic and
  Social Studies (181,025; 2.09%). The top-20 topics cover only a minority of works.

Finance vs Economics over time
- Finance's subfield share ran 9.69% (1950s) -> 10.46% -> 10.52% -> 10.09% -> 11.23% ->
  13.47% (2000s) -> 14.62% (2010s peak) -> 11.78% (2020s). Its rise is real but modest and
  has recently reversed.
- Economics and Econometrics mirrored this: ~84% (1950s) -> 74.55% low (2010s) -> 77.98%
  rebound (2020s), almost certainly a COVID-era surge in health/pandemic economics.

Forward (venue-based) leakage
- Journals whose names signal medicine/health/energy/environment total 640,423 works (7.41%).
  Top suspects: Medical Entomology and Zoology (47,135), Value in Health (19,950),
  Sustainability (7,755), JAMA (6,971), NEJM (5,521), AJN Nursing (5,520), Health Affairs
  (4,830), Energy Economics (4,715), J. Clinical Oncology (4,249), Am. J. Agricultural
  Economics (4,127). NOTE this probe both over-flags (the entomology glitch below, plus
  legitimately-economic Energy/Agricultural Economics) and under-flags (health works in
  neutrally-named venues).
- Five works that are plainly not economics: "Cancer statistics, 2020" (CA: A Cancer Journal
  for Clinicians, 2020, topic "Economic and Financial Impacts of Cancer", 21,415 cites);
  "Toxicity and response criteria of the ECOG" (Am. J. Clinical Oncology, 1982); "Reporting
  results of cancer treatment" (Cancer, 1981); "The socio-economic implications of COVID-19"
  (International Journal of Surgery, 2020); "Gastrointestinal Quality of Life Index"
  (British Journal of Surgery, 1995).

Topic-based leakage (cleaner lever)
- Summing works in unambiguously clinical/health primary_topics - Healthcare Policy and
  Management; Health Systems, Economic Evaluations, Quality of Life; COVID-19 Pandemic
  Impacts; HIV/AIDS Impact and Responses; Pharmaceutical Economics and Policy; Healthcare
  Systems and Reforms; Economic and Financial Impacts of Cancer - gives 723,166 works (8.36%).
- Combining suspect venues OR clear-health topics yields 1,152,831 works (13.33%).

Reverse concern (straddle topics)
- Three top-30 topics genuinely straddle economics and another discipline: Cinema and Media
  Studies (159,358; 1.84%), Healthcare Policy and Management (259,869; 3.01%), Climate Change
  Policy and Economics (101,777; 1.18%). Others - Diverse academic and cultural studies
  (116,251), Balkan and Eastern European Studies (103,202) - are area/humanities studies only
  loosely economic.

Two important caveats
- The 25% catch-all is NOT pure garbage: its highest-cited members are literally American
  Economic Review papers; its top venues mix nulls (284,082; 13%), university repositories
  (Columbia Academic Commons 62,070), OUP/Elsevier/Princeton eBooks, and oddities. It is a
  garbage magnet AND home to top-tier economics - so you cannot simply drop it.
- The single largest "suspect" journal, Medical Entomology and Zoology (47,135 works), is a
  METADATA GLITCH, not medical leakage: its actual titles are famous cross-disciplinary
  reference works - Baumol's "Contestable Markets and the Theory of Industry Structure", the
  "nlme" R package manual, the IPCC "Physical Science Basis", the "Wisconsin Card Sorting Test
  Manual" - spanning 1800-2024. OpenAlex mis-attached this one venue id to a pile of
  highly-cited books/reports.

BOTTOM-LINE CONTAMINATION ESTIMATE
- Hard, unambiguous non-economics leakage (clinical medicine, pure media/humanities, the
  entomology metadata artifact) is roughly 5-8% of the corpus.
- A broader definition that also excludes health-services/health-economics research pushes
  plausible non-core-economics to ~13.33%.

Filtering strategies (increasing aggressiveness)
 (a) VENUE blocklist for clinical journals (JAMA, NEJM, CA, Cancer, oncology/nursing/surgery
     titles): cheap, but 17.67% of works have NULL journal, and it misfires on the entomology
     glitch and on legitimate energy/agri-econ.
 (b) TOPIC filter dropping the ~7 clearly-clinical health topics (-8.36%), optionally plus
     media/area-studies topics: the most surgical lever.
 (c) SUBFIELD: too coarse (3 values) to help.
 RECOMMENDED: combine a topic-level exclusion list with a curated clinical-venue blocklist,
 and treat the 25% "Diverse Scientific and Economic Studies" bucket and the 17.67%
 null-journal slice as MANUAL-REVIEW zones rather than auto-dropping them.

Zero-author works are modern, not old
- 1,982,458 works (22.93%) have author_count = 0, but their median publication year is 2011;

- 8.12% of the 6,663,719 non-null/non-empty first_author values contain a comma

  (1970s), 9.9% (1980s), 5.1% (1990s), 5.0% (2000s), 6.0% (2010s), 10.5% (2020s).
- The 2020s rebound is driven by repository deposits: top comma-format 2020s sources are

10. OUTLIERS AND ODDITIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The best individual examples across dimensions (each is a concrete data-hygiene warning):

- MEDICAL ENTOMOLOGY AND ZOOLOGY (venue glitch). The #7 venue, 47,135 works spanning
  1800-2024, contains zero entomology: its actual titles are Baumol's "Contestable Markets",
  the "nlme" R manual, the IPCC "Physical Science Basis", and the "Wisconsin Card Sorting Test
  Manual". One venue id was mis-attached to a pile of highly-cited books/reports.

- 'MASTER, DANIEL M.' (author glitch). The 3rd most frequent first_author (14,709 works) is
  entirely a 2021-22 Harvard Dataverse bulk deposit of archaeology photo files (titles like
  "A97_8494.jpg"), all type=dataset, all misclassified as economics.

- "DIFFUSION OF INNOVATIONS 1" (citation/date glitch). The 2nd most-cited "economics" work
  (37,204 cites) is Rogers' communication-studies book carrying a bogus 2019 publication date.

- NORTH 1990 (duplication). "Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance"
  appears as two work ids (a Cambridge UP eBook and a Southern Economic Journal "article")
  splitting ~56,378 citations; de-duplicated it would be the 2nd most-cited work overall.

- HERRICK'S 1647 HESPERIDES (temporal artifact). The 222 works dated 1647 are Herrick's poems
  digitized as OUP eBook chapters (titles like "834. Upon Blinks. Epig.", "646. Physitians").
  The 1709-1712 spike is Tatler/Spectator issues; the 1727 spike (606 works) is a numbered
  Latin correspondence collection.

- 'TIME TO KNIT' (corrupt metadata). A "venue" of 7,799 works ALL dated 2000 whose titles are
  raw DOI strings - fully corrupt.

- 'WARFARE IN NORTH AMERICA, c. 1756-1815' (bulk dataset). 6,520 works, all type=dataset, all
  dated 2020: a digitized British National Archives catalogue (PRO 30/55, WO 34 references).

- 1970 REPOSITORY DUMP. The 1970-1971 -12.3% "drop" is an inflation of 1970 by repository
  default-dating (University of Patras 671 works dated 1970; Morehead State 590; vs 171 in
  1971).

- 2016 GERMAN BATCH. A one-year English dip in 2016 (65.6% vs ~72-74% adjacent) traces to a
  German spike (37,285 de works in 2016 vs 8,029 in 2015), of which 28,752 are book-chapters
  filed under the venue "Inactive DOIs" - a single batch-ingestion artifact.

- 'CANCER STATISTICS, 2020' (topic leakage). A CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians article
  with 21,415 citations shoehorned into topic "Economic and Financial Impacts of Cancer".

- 100-AUTHOR CONTRIBUTOR LISTS. The largest "teams" ("BOEKBESPREKINGEN" 1968, "Contributors
  to Volume 16" 1966) are contributor/abstract compilations at the 100-author truncation cap.


11. IMPLICATIONS FOR RESEARCH DESIGN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Filters to apply (baseline analytic sample)
- DROP author_count = 0 / first_author IS NULL for any authorship or team-size analysis
  (removes 1,982,458 rows, 22.93%; these are the same rows).
- DROP paratext + furniture-title works for substantive text/citation work: type = paratext
  OR the extended furniture-title patterns removes ~986,638 works (11.41%); the 7-pattern core
  alone is 473,537 (5.48%). Do NOT rely on type alone - 95,006 furniture-titled works
  (incl. 12,923 "Editorial Board" typed article) escape a type=paratext filter.
- RESTRICT temporal scope for substantive analyses to >= 1900, preferably >= 1940; treat
  pre-1900 (86,705 works, 1.0%) as classifier noise.
- TREAT recent years as incomplete: 2024-2025 counts are indexing lag, language is NULL for
  20.31% of 2025, and the 2021-22 dip is a type-specific ingestion artifact. Do not model
  post-2020 output trends from raw counts.
- FOR NON-ECON LEAKAGE: combine a topic-exclusion list (the ~7 clinical health topics,
  -8.36%; optionally media/area-studies) with a curated clinical-venue blocklist (JAMA, NEJM,
  CA, Cancer, oncology/nursing/surgery); leave the 25% "Diverse Scientific and Economic
  Studies" bucket and the 17.67% null-journal slice as manual-review zones. Expect to remove
  ~5-8% (hard leakage) to ~13.33% (broad) of the corpus.
- FLAG corrupt clusters for exclusion: the Medical Entomology and Zoology venue, 'Time to
  knit', 'Warfare in North America', 'Master, Daniel M.', 'Carey Hand Funeral Home'.

Columns to add from the raw archive (data/archive/) before specific analyses
- CITATION NETWORKS: re-extract referenced_works (the parquet keeps only the count, which is 0
  for 75.94% of rows). Also pull counts_by_year for citation dynamics.
- COLLABORATION / GEOGRAPHY: extract authorships[].institutions + countries (parquet has only
  a raw first_author string).
- AUTHOR CAREERS: extract authorships[].author.id (+ raw_orcid) for disambiguation - the
  first_author string cannot be disambiguated and mixes name formats.
- NORMALIZED IMPACT: extract fwci and citation_normalized_percentile; raw cited_by_count
  conflates cohort age with impact (median 0 every year, means fall mechanically with recency).
- VENUE IDENTITY: extract primary_location.source issn / host_organization / is_in_doaj to
  replace the ambiguous journal string (135,738 name variants, 170 "The X"/"X" duplicate pairs,
  cross-name splits like JAMA).
- FUNDING / OA DETAIL / TOPIC LISTS / biblio pages / is_retracted as needed per study.

Caveats for citation analyses specifically
- 70.81% of works are uncited (still 69.44% at maturity); use rank/percentile or FWCI, not raw
  means, and expect zero-inflated distributions.
- Citation concentration is extreme (top 1% = 53.39% of all citations); a handful of works
  dominate any aggregate.
- referenced_works_count = 0 for 75.94% of rows is a COVERAGE gap, not reference-free
  scholarship - do not interpret low reference counts substantively, and expect back-in-time
  coverage decay (1950s articles 95.9% zero-ref).
- De-duplicate at the publication level (North 1990 double-counting) and drop misclassified
  high-citation non-econ works (Rogers, Granovetter, Coleman, Cancer statistics) before
  ranking "top economics papers".
- Team-size/citation relationships are compositionally confounded (solo bucket carries more
  notes/comments/theses/local-language work); the author_count=0 bucket (avg 1.03 cites) must
  be excluded, not treated as "solo".

Caveats for text / NLP analyses
- Abstract presence (48.13%) is publisher-determined; Elsevier econ/finance journals are
  near-absent (4-10% coverage) while OUP/T&F are ~96%. Document this selection bias and
  consider re-extracting abstract_inverted_index from the archive.
- Language filters silently drop recent, especially 2025, records (20.31% NULL language).

13. LIMITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- SCOPE OF DEFINITION. The corpus is defined by OpenAlex primary_topic.field = 20. Because
  OpenAlex's topic classifier misfires, the corpus both admits non-economics work (~5-13%
  depending on strictness) and would omit economics work whose PRIMARY topic OpenAlex assigned
  elsewhere - the latter is not measurable from this extract.
- 17-OF-49 PROJECTION. The parquet drops ~32 raw fields, including the citation edges,
  affiliations, disambiguated author IDs, and normalized-impact metrics. Several headline
  caveats (reference coverage, network feasibility) reflect the projection, not OpenAlex's
  underlying data; re-extraction from data/archive/ can recover them.
- ARCHIVE PRESENCE RATES ARE UPPER BOUNDS. The unextracted-field presence rates in Section 4
  come from the head-of-file sample of one year's archive (first 2,000 records of
  econ_2020.jsonl.gz), which skews toward well-indexed records (doi 98.3% in-sample vs 74.4%
  for all of 2020). Treat them as indicative maxima, not corpus-wide rates.
- METADATA-LAG CONTAMINATES RECENT YEARS. 2024-2025 work counts, language coverage, and
  citation/reference accumulation are all depressed by indexing lag; recent-edge trends are
  unreliable.
- CITATION AND REFERENCE COUNTS ARE COVERAGE-BOUND. Both cited_by_count and
  referenced_works_count reflect OpenAlex's parsing coverage, which decays going back in time
  and is thin for non-mainstream venues; absolute levels understate true citation activity.
- NO PUBLICATION-LEVEL DE-DUPLICATION. The row grain is the OpenAlex work id; the same
  intellectual object can appear multiple times (North 1990) and the same journal under
  multiple name strings (JAMA), inflating both work counts and venue statistics until cleaned.
- QUALITATIVE SUB-CLAIMS. A small number of narrative sub-claims (e.g. specific titles behind
  a glitched venue, "lowest since 2012") were confirmed consistent with reproduced numbers but
  not independently re-queried; all load-bearing quantitative values were re-verified.
